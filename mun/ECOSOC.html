Hiya! I’m Dakshina Chetti and I will be one of the two chairs taking care of the ECOSOC committee this year. I have been an MUN delegate for the past 6 years, and an MUN Chair for the past four, and my school’s Deputy Secretary-General for the past year. If anyone in my committee has any questions about anything (life and the universe—MUN too), feel free to contact me. :)

Topic Area Summaries

The question of GMOs and patented seeds in relation to food security

What’s the sitch? With the advent of genetic sciences and the discovery of gene vectors, scientists and corporations have made great use of GMO’s, otherwise known as genetically-modified organisms. Whilst they have had some positive impacts, such as with the production of “Golden Rice” with the beta-carotene gene to help combat dietary shortages of vitamin-A in poverty-stricken areas, GMO’s have come under fire—especially in the last decade. GMO’s produce higher yields, are often insect-resistant, and are frequently used in the agricultural industry—however, the rapid growth of GMO plants on agricultural land is having serious repercussions of biodiversity, as well as causing long-term damage to agricultural soil. Additionally, issues of food-safety have been raised. Thus, GMO’s are a considerable source of debate and disagreement in the international community; causing panic amongst governments right down to the industries that produce said organisms.

The problem today with patented seeds is they give the “creator” of the produce the opportunity to commercialize their invention and to acquire a return on their commitment of time and money. If plant breeders are not able to commercialize on their produce, there is less of an incentive for them to work at generating new breeds of plants.
